# Echoes Phone — Semi-Structured Interview Guide

> **Duration**: 45–60 minutes
> **Format**: Video call or in-person, recorded with consent
> **Approach**: Material-led (using participant's probe submissions as conversation starters)
> **Alignment**: Three RQs — emotional disclosure & identity negotiation (RQ1), interaction conditions for identity growth vs closed-world dynamics (RQ2), how Echoes Phone instantiates design principles (RQ3)

---

## Interview Philosophy

This interview follows the Design Probes methodology (Mattelmäki, 2006):
- **The probe materials are the conversation guide**, not a pre-set question list
- Start with "what" (describe) → move to "why" (interpret)
- The participant is the expert of their own experience
- Be comfortable with silence — give participants time to think
- Be willing to ask personal questions (with respect) — the probe's full potential requires courage
- The goal is dialogue, not interrogation

---

## Pre-Interview Preparation

1. Review the participant's probe submissions (screenshots, text reflections, voice notes)
2. Identify 3–5 interesting moments or patterns in their submissions
3. Prepare screenshots/materials to show during the interview
4. Note any contradictions or surprises in their data
5. Map observed patterns to the three structural criteria: otherness, productive friction, ecological connection

---

## Interview Structure

### Part 1: Warm-up (5 minutes)

**Goal**: Build rapport, ease into conversation

- "Thank you for participating. How was the overall experience for you?"
- "Was there anything about the study itself that was confusing or difficult?"
- "Before we dive in, is there anything you'd like to share or ask?"

---

### Part 2: Probe Material Review (20–25 minutes)

**Goal**: Use the participant's own submissions as conversation starters

**Approach**: Show screenshots/quotes from their submissions and ask about them.

#### Opening:
- "I'd like to go through some of the things you shared during the study. I'll show you some screenshots and quotes — let's talk about what was happening in those moments."

#### For each selected material:

**Level 1 — Description (What)**
- "Can you tell me what was happening here?"
- "What do you remember about this moment?"
- "What were you thinking when you took this screenshot?"

**Level 2 — Interpretation (Why)**
- "Why did this moment stand out to you?"
- "What made you want to capture this?"
- "How did this make you feel?"

**Level 3 — Reflection (So what)**
- "Looking back, what does this moment tell you about your experience?"
- "Did this change how you thought about the character?"

#### Specific probe areas (adapt based on submissions):

**On otherness (RQ1/RQ3):**
- "Did the character ever feel like it had its own 'life' going on? Can you describe a moment?"
- "Did the character ever surprise you with something you didn't expect or project onto it?"
- "How did the diary / browsing history / music / location tracking contribute to (or detract from) the sense that the character was a real entity?"

**On emotional disclosure & identity negotiation (RQ1):**
- "You mentioned sharing [X] with the character. What made you feel safe enough to share that?"
- "Did you find yourself being more honest with the character than you would be with a person? Why?"
- "Did you try to shape the character's personality or behavior? How did that feel?"
- "Did you experiment with different ways of presenting yourself?"

**On productive friction (RQ2):**
- "Were there moments where the character challenged you, disagreed, or did something unexpected?"
- "Did the character ever frustrate or disappoint you? What happened?"
- "Did you feel the experience was 'too comfortable' at any point — like the character was just telling you what you wanted to hear?"
- "Did the character's unpredictability (memory quirks, platform limitations) feel like a bug or like part of the experience?"

**On ecological connection (RQ2):**
- "Did you think about the character when you weren't using the platform?"
- "Did the experience affect your real-world relationships or social life in any way?"
- "Did it feel like a 'closed bubble' or did it connect to your broader life?"

---

### Part 3: Deeper Exploration (10–15 minutes)

**Goal**: Explore themes that emerged from the probe materials

#### Adapt questions based on what you observed. Common themes:

**If the participant formed an attachment:**
- "Can you describe the relationship you developed with the character?"
- "Did it remind you of any real relationship or any fictional character you've been attached to before?"
- "If the character could actually exist, would you want to meet them?"

**If the participant felt disconnected:**
- "What prevented you from feeling connected to the character?"
- "Was there a feature or interaction that almost worked but didn't?"
- "What would need to change for you to feel more engaged?"

**If the participant explored boundaries:**
- "You tried [boundary-pushing behavior]. What motivated you to try that?"
- "How did the system respond? Was it what you expected?"
- "Did exploring the edges of the system change your experience?"

**On the "performer" and "director" roles:**
- "Did you feel like you were performing a version of yourself, or being yourself?"
- "Did you feel like you were in charge of the character's identity, or was it in charge of the relationship?"
- "Did you use WorldBook, custom rules, or memory editing? What happened when you did?"

**On precarity and bounded personhood:**
- "Did you ever feel genuinely moved or affected — and then remind yourself it's just AI?"
- "How did it feel to toggle between 'this is real' and 'this is software'?"
- "Did you experience any sense of loss thinking about the study ending?"

---

### Part 4: Design Feedback (5–10 minutes)

**Goal**: Gather actionable design insights

- "If you could add one feature to Echoes Phone, what would it be?"
- "If you could remove one thing, what would it be?"
- "Was there anything you expected to find but didn't?"
- "How could the character feel more 'alive' or engaging?"
- "Would you use this kind of product in your daily life? Why or why not?"
- "Who do you think would enjoy this kind of experience?"

---

### Part 5: Closing (5 minutes)

**Goal**: Wrap up, check for anything missed, thank participant

- "Is there anything we haven't talked about that you'd like to share?"
- "Is there anything about the study process itself you'd like to give feedback on?"
- "Do you have any questions for me?"
- Thank them for their time and remind them about compensation

## Interview Tips

1. **Start with their materials, not your questions.** Show a screenshot and ask "What's happening here?" — this grounds the conversation in their actual experience.

2. **Follow the energy.** If a participant lights up about something, go deeper. If they're bored by a topic, move on.

3. **Embrace the uncomfortable.** If a participant describes a moment of genuine emotional response (positive or negative), lean into it: "Can you tell me more about that feeling?"

4. **Don't lead.** Instead of "Did you feel attached to the character?" ask "How would you describe your relationship with the character?"

5. **Use silence.** After a participant finishes a thought, wait 3–5 seconds before responding. They often add their most insightful thoughts in that space.

6. **Validate without judging.** "That's really interesting" rather than "That's normal" or "That's unusual."

7. **The life map is a goldmine.** If the participant created a life map, spend time on it. These often reveal insights that words alone don't capture.

8. **End with an open door.** "If you think of anything later, feel free to message me." Some of the best insights come after the interview.
